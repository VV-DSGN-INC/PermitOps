# Botpress Founder Round — Prep

The final step in the Botpress loop: recruiter (done) → Director of Product + PM → team meet-and-greet (Amber, Jung-one) → **founder/CEO**. Recruiter's exact framing:

> "He's very hands-on. He's an engineer himself, but we still would like to see if the vision is aligned there in terms of the brand's future and its designs."

You already opened a door with him in round 1 — *"I saw his GitHub comics. It's inspiring when the leader is actually building the product."* That instinct is right. This round rewards peer-to-peer builder energy, not a design-leadership pitch.

---

## Why this round is unlike the others

| Prior rounds | Founder round |
|---|---|
| Can you do the job / is the handover safe | Is your **vision** for design + brand aligned with mine |
| Non-designers, speak product/business | An **engineer who made the design calls himself** — he can verify craft and smell process-talk |
| Reactive Q&A | He wants a **point of view**, pushed back on, held under pressure |

Three things an engineer-founder respects and three he doesn't:

**Respects:** technical fluency (you build, not just spec) · taste he can verify on his own product · strong opinions, loosely held.
**Allergic to:** "design maturity" lectures · design-as-service / "let me bring real design" (he built what exists — that framing insults it) · asking for a seat at the table (Berkun's "no one cares" — arrive with solved problems).

---

## Your decisive card (lead with it, don't bury it)

You have something almost no design candidate can offer an engineer-CEO: **you've built the thing his company sells.** From the vibefolio — you've shipped a node-based visual flow-builder for AI pipelines (React Flow, typed handles, topological executor), your own AI-agent command-and-observability tooling, and you cloned `botpress/botpress` as a reference.

To an engineer-founder, that converts you from *"a designer evaluating my product"* to *"a peer who has solved this exact problem and read my codebase."* Say it plainly, early:

> "Before we go further — I should say I'm not just a designer who'd use Botpress. I've built a node-based flow-builder for AI pipelines myself, I run my own agent command-and-observability tooling, and I cloned your repo to read how you structure bots and integrations. So when I talk about agent-builder UX, it's from having made the same hard calls your team made — node density, typed connections, where the code escape hatch lives."

This single move reframes the entire conversation. Use it in the first five minutes.

## Questions to expect — with answers (headline-first, then ≤3 beats, stop)

### 1. "Why Botpress / why this role?" (vision-level, not logistics)
> "Agent-builder UX is the design frontier — the patterns aren't set, and whoever sets them in the next 18 months defines how non-developers build with AI for a decade. I've been pointed at this for fourteen years: founding designer at Acies.ai, AI work at Sprig, and on my own I ship AI-native tools weekly, including a flow-builder that's essentially your core problem. Botpress is the rare place where that problem *is* the company, the team's lean enough that design decides outcomes, and the founder still builds. That last part matters to me — I work best with people who make things, not just review them."

### 2. "What's your vision for design and brand at Botpress as we scale?"
The question the recruiter flagged. Have a real POV — three beats:
> "One: the product has to *be* AI-native, not a database UI with a chat box bolted on — that's the difference between you and the incumbents, and it's a design problem, not a feature list. Two: the dual audience — developers and non-technical users — is the hardest design problem you have and the moat if you get it right; I'd hold both without dumbing either down, which is exactly the InRule problem I've shipped. Three: trust and legibility in agent-building — people don't trust agents they can't see; making the system legible is brand *and* product. On brand specifically: at your stage brand follows product truth, it doesn't lead it — I'd make the product unmistakably yours before I'd touch a visual refresh."

### 3. "What do you think of our product? What would you change?" (craft + judgment — he can verify)
Do not wing this. Build an agent in Studio first. Then, Budd's lens:
> "I spent [N] hours building [specific agent] and cloned the repo. Before I call anything a flaw, I asked what it's a *correct* consequence of — you're competing on extensibility and speed, so some rough edges are right. Two I'd actually change because they cost activation for the non-technical user: [specific], [specific]. One I'd leave because it's load-bearing for the developer audience: [specific]. First 30 days I'd ship the first fix myself as a visible IC change, not a deck."

### 4. "How do you think about AI agent UX?" (he'll go deep — he's technical)
> "The constraint isn't the model, it's legibility and control. I learned this building my own agent tooling — I couldn't trust a squad of agents I couldn't see, so I built observability for them instead of trusting a chat window. Same in product: the three unsolved problems are confidence/uncertainty without paralysis, tool-use confirmation that's neither noisy nor permissive, and memory that's useful without feeling surveilled. Most products solve one and ignore two. The Botpress opportunity is making the legible-and-controllable version the default, at platform scale, so every builder inherits it."

### 5. "We move fast and design has been everyone's job. Why should it be owned now?" (he made the calls — tread carefully)
> "Because what you built got you to 115 people — that's evidence it was good enough, not that it should stay distributed. The reason to own it now isn't 'design was bad,' it's leverage: at this size, undecided design patterns get re-litigated in every feature, and that tax compounds. My job isn't to replace your decisions — it's to turn the ones that work into a system the team reuses, so velocity goes up, not down. You keep the vision; I make it consistent and faster."

### 6. "How technical are you / how do you work with engineers?" (he is one)
> "Technical enough to be useful and to know what I'm asking for. I clone repos to understand products. I prototype in code-adjacent tools and I've shipped real apps — Next.js, AI SDK, React Flow. At Acies.ai I designed the first report builder to exactly what the backend could return that quarter, shipped, then we expanded it. I don't hand engineers fantasies and I don't need a PM to translate. With strong engineers I work live; I adapt the ceremony, never the spine."

### 7. "What does great look like 12 months in?"
> "Three things, measurable. The product reads as AI-native to a first-time non-technical user — they reach a working agent fast and trust it. There's a design system the team extends without me in the loop, so design isn't the bottleneck. And Amber and Jung-one are visibly leveled up — one operating a band higher than today. If those three are true, design stopped being a tax and became a multiplier — and you'd feel it in ship speed, not in a portfolio."

### 8. Founder curveballs (he may throw one)
- *"What's a product you admire and why?"* → pick one with a strong technical-taste reason (Linear: opinionated, fast, design-led, no settings-sprawl). Not "it's beautiful."
- *"Tell me something I'm wrong about / what's your strongest opinion?"* → "Most agent platforms are betting on more automation. I think the next win is legibility — the platform that makes agents *auditable* by non-developers wins the enterprise, not the one with the most blocks. I'd bet design there."
- *"Convince me design matters here."* → don't argue it abstractly; "I won't — I'll show you. Give me the support-product flow and I'll walk you through where trust is won or lost in it. That's design deciding the metric, not decorating it."

## Landmines (founder-specific)

- ❌ "I'd bring real design / design maturity" — he built it; this reads as an insult and as consultant-speak.
- ❌ Process lectures (critique cadence, ops) — save for the team; founders want vision + craft.
- ❌ Hedged opinions — this is the one round where a strong, structured opinion beats a balanced one. (Still headline-first; just don't "it depends.")
- ❌ Rambling — the persistent issue. With a founder the fix isn't shorter, it's *structured conviction*: claim → why → evidence → stop.
- ❌ Pretending to know his codebase deeper than you do — say "I read enough of the repo to understand X," not fake depth. He'll test it.

## Questions YOU ask the founder

Founder-grade, not logistics (logistics were for the recruiter):

- "What's the design decision you made early that you're proudest of — and the one you'd undo now?"
- "Where do you most feel the cost of design being everyone's job today?"
- "Eighteen months out — is the bet that non-technical users build agents themselves, or that they get built for them and users mostly operate them?"
- "What would make you regret hiring a Head of UX a year from now?"
- "What's the part of the product you'd defend even if every designer told you it was wrong — and why?"

That last one is a founder's favorite question to be asked. It signals you respect that he has product conviction, which de-risks the "will this person fight me" subtext.
