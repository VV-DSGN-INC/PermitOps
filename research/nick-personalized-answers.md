### 4. What's your design philosophy?
> "Three principles. **Clarity over cleverness** — if the interface needs explanation, the design isn't done. **The user's hardest moment is your design brief** — most teams design the happy path; I design the error state first. At Acies.ai, the dashboard had to work when the data was incomplete, contradictory, and showed a fraud spike at 3am — that's where the design lives. **Make the AI disappear** — when I'm designing AI features, the best version is the one where the model isn't the hero. At Sprig that means the AI drafts the survey but the researcher feels like they did it themselves."

### 8. How do you run design critique?
Lean on your actual experience and the Acies.ai in-room context:
> "Weekly, sixty minutes, max four designers, predictable cadence. Format I'd run: each designer brings one piece in progress, five minutes of context, fifteen minutes of feedback. I go last so I don't anchor the room. Rules: feedback addresses the work not the person, alternatives must be specific not 'I'd just…,' and the designer owns whether to adopt the feedback. The reason I'm specific about this is at Acies.ai I worked literally in-room with the CTO and CEO and the engineering team — critique was constant and unstructured, and I learned the hard way that without structure, the loudest person wins. Structured critique is how juniors get to speak."

The Acies.ai detail is unique to you and lands the "I've earned this opinion" signal.

### 9. What's your view on AI / agent UX right now? (Sprig-anchored)
> "We're at the moment where the medium is wider than the language to describe it. The patterns that will define the next decade are being set in the next 18 months. The unsolved problems I care most about — directly from my Sprig work — are: how do you communicate confidence and uncertainty without paralyzing the user; how do you design tool-use confirmation that's neither too noisy nor too permissive; and how do you handle the moment the AI gets it wrong. Most products today solve one of those three and ignore the other two. At Sprig I'm working on the second one specifically — when the AI drafts a survey, the moment the researcher disagrees with the draft is the design problem. Botpress at platform scale has the same three problems, multiplied across every agent your customers ship."

This is your *strongest* answer in the deck. Memorize it. It earns you the room.

### 10. How would you design for hallucinations in the UI?
> "Layered disclosure. Three tiers — confident with source, confident without source, low-confidence with caveat. Different visual treatments for each. At Sprig the equivalent problem is the AI drafting a survey question that's biased or leading; the design move there is surfacing the source — what prior question or upload influenced the draft — rather than slapping a confidence number on it. Source beats confidence-bar in almost every case. For a builder tool like Botpress, the additional layer is teaching the *builder* to design their agent's hallucination behavior: when to fall back, when to escalate, when to refuse. The UX for managing hallucinations starts at agent design time, not run time."

Pull-through from Sprig + extend to Botpress = senior signal.

### 11. How do you extend a design system to handle AI patterns?

Confidence and source patterns: citation chips, hedge language tokens, fallback states. Agent control surfaces: tool permission cards, memory viewers, conversation thread management. Most existing systems have zero of these. Combined with the Maven Design System Bootcamp I did in 2023, this is the part of the role I feel most ready to own from day one."
