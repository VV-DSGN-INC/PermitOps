# Head of UX / UX Director Interview Prep — Botpress (Montreal)

Target role: **Head of UX, Botpress** — fully extensible AI agent / chatbot platform, fully in-office Montreal, ~500K users, lean & AI-leveraged startup.

Source job posting: https://uiuxjobsboard.com/job/1408426-head-of-ux-montreal-canada

---

## 0. Role decoded — Player-coach with juniors

**Confirmed scope: hands-on IC + managing junior designers.** This is the classic startup "player-coach" pattern. It's the hardest role in design leadership and the one most people fail at — for predictable reasons we'll address.

**Signals from the JD:**
- **AI-first product** — "complex interaction models for AI-first workflows, including agents and LLM capabilities"
- **Design system ownership** — "Build and maintain design systems, particularly for AI-driven interactions"
- **Modern AI prototyping** — explicit: Claude Code, V0
- **Process** — "establish research feedback loops and design critique standards"
- **Bias toward principles** — "principles for clarity in high-stakes product flows"

**Why player-coach + juniors is the hardest configuration:**
- You out-skill your reports, so the fastest path on any task is to just do it yourself → you become the bottleneck
- Juniors learn by watching, then trying, then being critiqued — you have to be **visible at the craft** while leaving space for them to fail safely
- Your time fragments: shipping work, unblocking juniors, hiring, design system, stakeholder mgmt — without a senior IC to lean on
- You'll be judged on **two scoreboards**: the work you personally shipped AND the growth trajectory of your reports

**The single sentence to land in interview:**
> "I scale myself through my team. The juniors are the leverage, not the load. My job is to model the craft on the most ambiguous work, run the critique that makes them better, and protect their focus while I take the political hits."

**Two questions to still clarify in interview #1:**
1. **What's the IC / coaching / management time split today?** (Healthy: roughly 50/30/20 or 60/20/20. Red flag: anything where coaching is <20%.)
2. **How many juniors, at what level, and what's the headcount plan?** (1–3 juniors is coachable. 4+ with no mid-level between you = burnout factory.)

## 0.5. Player-coach operating model — the answers you need ready

You will be asked at least 3–4 questions in this category. Here are pre-built positions to draw from.

### Time allocation
Default target: **~55% IC ship work, ~25% coaching/critique, ~20% strategy/hiring/stakeholder.**

The IC % stays high specifically because:
- Juniors learn by watching you do the work
- AI/agent UX is too new for hand-off — you're inventing the patterns
- A lean startup can't afford a "manager who codes nothing"

The % drifts toward coaching as juniors level up and as you hire a mid-level. **Stating that you'll let the ratio shift is more credible than committing to a fixed split.**

### Which work I take vs. delegate
| I take | I delegate to a junior |
|---|---|
| The ambiguous 0→1 problem with no precedent | The well-scoped iteration on a known surface |
| The piece that sets a pattern the team will reuse | The piece that uses an existing pattern |
| The exec-visibility design that lives or dies on craft | The piece where "good enough + ship" beats "perfect + late" |
| The thing the junior would copy badly | The thing where a different approach than mine is fine |

### Critique format (rehearsed answer)
> "Weekly, 60 min, max 4 designers. Each shares one piece in progress, 5 min context + 15 min feedback. I go last. Rules: feedback addresses the work not the person, alternatives must be specific not 'I'd just…', and the designer owns whether to adopt the feedback. I document themes for the next session — patterns that came up tell us what to teach."

### How I avoid becoming the bottleneck
1. **Office hours twice a week** — juniors batch questions instead of interrupting
2. **A "default direction" memo** for any ambiguous area — juniors decide without me until they hit something explicitly listed
3. **Make my reasoning visible** — short looms or written rationales on my own work, so juniors see how I make calls
4. **One promotion target per junior every 6 months** — explicit, written, dated
5. **Hire a mid-level fast** — the second hire is the leverage hire

### Failure modes I watch for in myself
- Doing the junior's revision rather than coaching the revision
- Critique becoming a monologue
- Skipping critique because I'm "too busy shipping"
- IC drift — taking the fun ambiguous work and leaving juniors with cleanup
- Stakeholder-shielding so much that juniors never learn to defend their own work

## 1. Leadership / strategy questions you will be asked

### Vision & strategy
- What's your design philosophy?
- How would you describe your design thinking?
- How do you plan to advocate for UX? Give me an example.
- How would you convince leadership and stakeholders of the value of design?
- How do you measure success in UX?
- What does the ideal design process look like?
- What's the difference between great design and good design at our stage?

### People management & hiring
- What kind of manager are you?
- What's your mentorship process?
- How do you see building out the team in the next 1–3 years?
- How would you handle a difficult employee?
- How do you keep designers engaged?
- What do you value in designers and researchers?
- What kind of people do you especially like to work with? Have a hard time with?
- How do you handle giving constructive feedback?
- Describe a time you resolved conflict within your team.

### Player-coach specific (managing juniors while shipping)
- How do you split your time between your own design work and coaching?
- How do you decide which work you take vs. give to a junior?
- A junior is stuck on a design at 9pm before a ship date. Do you take it over, pair with them, or let them ship something B-grade?
- How do you run critique with juniors without crushing them?
- What's your leveling rubric? How do you decide when a junior is ready for promotion?
- How do you onboard a new junior designer?
- A junior repeatedly misses the bar on craft. Walk me through what happens over 90 days.
- How do you give a junior a stretch project without setting them up to fail?
- Tell me about a junior you helped grow. What did you change about them?
- Tell me about a junior who didn't work out. What did you learn?
- How do you avoid becoming the bottleneck when you're the most senior designer?
- How do you teach taste without dictating taste?
- How do you do hands-on work in a way that scales — i.e., your IC work makes the team better, not just the product?

### Stakeholder & cross-functional
- How would you work with Engineering and Product day-to-day?
- Tell me about a time a client/PM disagreed with your team's design approach — how did you resolve it?
- How do you prioritize when product, engineering, and design all push different priorities?
- How do you keep design influential without becoming a bottleneck?
- Describe a time you had to say no to an idea.

### Operations
- How do you run design critique?
- How do you ship quickly without sacrificing craft?
- What's your approach to research feedback loops?
- Name some tools or processes you use to unblock designers.
- What's your experience managing/mentoring designers, and how do you delegate?

### Self-awareness
- What gives you the most energy? What drains you?
- What do you need from your manager to be successful?
- What's one of the best ideas you've had? The worst?
- Why are you leaving your current role?
- What will you do in your first 90 days?

### Situational (STAR)
- Walk me through your design process on a complex project from ideation to ship.
- Tell me about a project that had to pivot mid-flight — what did you do?
- Tell me about a project where you had to compress timeline without losing quality.
- Describe a design crisis you handled.
- Tell me about a project that failed — what did you learn?

## 2. AI / agent UX questions specific to Botpress

Because the product is a chatbot/agent **builder** (not just a chatbot), interviewers will probe both consumer-facing AI UX *and* the meta-UX of designing tools that other people use to build AI experiences.

### Conversational & agent UX
- How do you design for streaming responses? What changes vs. instant feedback?
- How do you handle hallucinations in the UI — what's your taxonomy of disclosure, confidence, and recovery?
- How do you communicate cost/latency tradeoffs to end users?
- Design an interface where the agent has access to tools (e.g., calendar, email). How do you handle permission, confirmation, and revocation?
- How do you handle long conversations that exceed context window limits?
- What's your view on suggested replies vs. open-ended input?
- How would you design the failure state when the model says "I don't know"?
- Walk us through how you'd add memory to a conversational UX without spooking the user.

### Builder / tooling UX (the Botpress lens)
- How do you design a "no-code" agent builder for non-technical users without dumbing it down for developers?
- What patterns do you use for visual flow editors (Botpress, Voiceflow, Rasa, n8n, etc.)?
- How do you balance visual canvas vs. code escape hatch?
- How do you design observability — logs, traces, evaluation — into a builder tool?
- Critique Botpress Studio. What's working, what's not, what would you change in your first 90 days?
- How would you design the agent-testing experience? (eval, replay, A/B)

### Design systems for AI
- How do you extend an existing design system to handle AI-specific patterns (streaming, citations, confidence indicators, agent state)?
- What new components would you add? Which existing ones break?
- How do you handle non-deterministic UI — when the same input may produce a different visual response?

## 4. Portfolio presentation tactics for design-leader roles

**Choose 2 case studies. Go deep.** Don't fly through 6.

### Story arc (per case)
1. Business problem (with a number — revenue, retention, time-to-task, NPS)
2. Team context — size, your role, your peers, stakeholders
3. Process — research, critique, principles, tradeoffs
4. The hard moment — what almost broke it
5. Outcome — quant + qual + lessons learned

### Balance to demonstrate
- **Team ops AND product innovation** — process + outcomes
- **Strategic vision AND tactical execution** — zoom in and out
- **Business impact AND user needs** — both metrics
- **T-shaped** — breadth across hiring/strategy/critique, with one depth area
- **Leading from front AND behind** — when you stepped up, when you held back

### Always credit the team
> "Three designers, two researchers, one PM, and an engineer. I owned the strategy and the flows for the agent builder. Maria led the visual system."

### Avoid
- Reading from notes
- "It's just a small project…"
- Showing IC work without the leadership lens
- Buzzwords without examples
- Solo-hero narrative

## 5. 30-60-90 day plan to bring to the final interview

Hiring managers love this. **~70% of design leaders who present one get a stronger offer signal** (LinkedIn Talent Trends). Frame it through the **player-coach** lens — your plan must show both shipping work AND growing the juniors.

### Days 0–30 — Listen + ship one small thing
- 1:1s with every designer (deep), every PM, every eng lead, the founder
- For each junior: skill assessment, growth ambitions, current blockers, last 3 projects critiqued together
- Customer calls (target 20)
- Audit: design system, research repo, critique cadence, design tools
- Audit: 3–5 product flows end-to-end as a real user
- **Ship one small visible piece of IC work myself** — proves the craft, sets the bar, gives juniors a model to study
- Map the org's design maturity

### Days 31–60 — Diagnose & frame
- Share back what I heard: 3 problems, 3 strengths
- Propose principles for agent / AI UX
- **Stand up weekly critique** with a documented format — this is the single highest-leverage ritual for juniors
- Identify 1 quick win to ship — **led by a junior with me as coach**, not by me solo
- Define how design success will be measured (both shipping AND junior growth metrics)
- Lightweight leveling rubric so promotions stop being political

### Days 61–90 — Commit
- Ship the junior-led quick win
- Propose the 12-month roadmap for design ops + design system + research
- Hiring plan: a **mid-level** designer to take pressure off me, before more juniors
- One bold proposal — something I would not have shipped before week 8
- For each junior: a stretch project queued up with explicit growth goals

**Caveat in the presentation:** "This is a hypothesis. The first 30 days will probably change it — and that's the point."

**Why the day-30 IC ship matters in a player-coach role:** new design leaders who only listen for 90 days lose credibility with juniors who need to see the bar; new design leaders who only ship lose credibility with everyone else. Doing one small IC ship in month one buys you the right to coach.

## 6. Questions YOU should ask (red-flag detection)

Ask the SAME question across hiring manager, peers, and team — listen for inconsistencies.

### Role definition
- Is this a new role or a backfill? If backfill, why did the last person leave?
- What's the realistic IC / coaching / management time split — today and in 12 months?
- How many designers report into me, at what level? (juniors only, or any mid/senior?)
- Why wasn't this role filled internally?
- Who does this role report to, and how often?
- What's the bar on hiring? Can I bring in a mid-level early to relieve coaching load?

### Design team health
- How big is the design team? What's the headcount plan?
- What's the team's biggest challenge from the past year?
- How does design relate to product and engineering?
- Which team currently has the most influence over design decisions?
- What do PM and engineering leads expect from a design peer?

### Business & strategy
- Who's the primary competitor — are we leading or chasing?
- What's the timeline from concept to launch on the most recent shipped feature?
- What's the biggest business challenge in the next 12 months?
- Is there a Chief Design / Product Officer on the exec team? If not, who carries the design voice?

### Hiring manager
- How do you handle organizational pressure?
- What's your tenure, and has the role lived up to your ambitions?
- If things go sideways in three months, what most likely happened?
- What does progression look like from here?

### AI-specific (Botpress-specific)
- How is design represented when model behavior changes (new LLM, latency shift)?
- How much of the roadmap is driven by enterprise customer asks vs. platform vision?
- What's the build vs. buy stance on internal AI tooling — and who owns AI in design?
- How do you measure success of an AI feature today?

## 7. Likely interview-loop shape (Head-of-UX / Director, ~5 rounds)

1. **Recruiter screen** — 30 min — motivations, comp, timing
2. **Hiring manager** — 45–60 min — story, philosophy, behavioral
3. **Portfolio review** — 60–90 min — 2 case studies, deep dive
4. **Cross-functional panel** — 45 min — PM + engineering peer, working style
5. **Executive / founder** — 45 min — vision, fit, ambition, why-this-company
6. *(Sometimes)* On-site exercise or take-home — strategic memo or product critique
7. **Reverse pitch / 30-60-90** — 30 min — your plan for the role
