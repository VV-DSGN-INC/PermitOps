## A. About You / Background (1–8)

### 1. Tell me about yourself.
A 90-second arc with a through-line that lands at AI agent UX:
> "I started in [discipline], moved into product design at [company] because I cared more about how things worked than how they looked. The decision that defined my career was [pivotal moment — first lead role / first AI project / first time managing]. Over the last [X] years I've shipped [N] products and grown [N] designers. What pulled me toward Botpress is that agent UX is the most consequential design problem of this decade, and Botpress is the rare company where design owns the surface that everyone else builds on top of."

Don't recite a resume. Pick three projects worth a story and skip the rest. End with conviction about *this* role, not your past.

### 2. Why design? What's your origin story?
> "I was the kid who took things apart. The thing that hooked me on design specifically — not engineering, not art — was realizing that interfaces are the place where someone's intent meets a system's capability. When that meeting goes badly, blame falls on the user. When it goes well, the system disappears. I want to build the disappearing kind."

Avoid the "I love beautiful things" trap. For a senior role, link your origin to a *systems* sensibility.

### 3. Why Botpress? Why now?
Three sentences, three angles:
> "First, agent UX is the design frontier — the patterns are unsettled, and the people who set them in the next 18 months will define how non-technical humans interact with AI for a decade. Second, Botpress is positioned uniquely: most competitors are either no-code-only (Voiceflow) or developer-only (Rasa); the bet on being both is bold and design-led. Third, I'm at the point in my career where I want to own a surface, not contribute to one — Head of UX at this stage of Botpress is exactly that."

Reference *their* product, not generic AI hype.

### 4. Why are you leaving your current role?
Pull toward, don't push from:
> "I'm leaving toward something specific, not away from anything. At [current company] I shipped [X] and grew the team to [N]. The work is good. But the design problem in front of Botpress is bigger and weirder than what I have access to today, and I want to be in the room where those patterns get invented."

Never trash a past employer. Never let the answer last more than 45 seconds.

### 5. Where do you see yourself in 5 years?
> "Honest answer: I want to be the person responsible for how a generation of non-developers builds with AI. Whether that's at Botpress, in a research role, or running design for an AI infrastructure company — that's the work. So 5 years from now: deeper at this layer, broader scope, and ideally still doing some IC work. I lose myself if I stop touching the craft."

Show ambition tied to *the work*, not the title. Mention staying hands-on — they want that.

### 6. What are your biggest strengths as a design leader?
Pick three, anchor each in a moment:
> "One — I take ambiguous problems and make them tractable. At [company] I inherited a project everyone called 'unsalvageable' and the move I made was reframing the problem from [X] to [Y]; it shipped in [N] months. Two — I scale myself through critique. Designers who work with me get measurably better at defending their work, and I have specific examples. Three — I keep shipping. I don't have an IC-to-management cliff."

Three, not five. Specific moments, not adjectives.

### 7. What's your biggest weakness?
The honest version that's still hireable:
> "I default to taking on too much IC work when a project is in trouble, because shipping is the muscle I'm most confident in. The cost is that juniors miss the stretch experience. I've learned to catch myself, and the rule I use is: if I'm about to step in, can I instead pair for 30 minutes and let them ship it? Most of the time the answer is yes. I'm not fully cured — but I see it now."

Real weakness + active correction. Never "I'm a perfectionist."

### 8. What gives you energy? What drains you?
> "Energizes me: ambiguous problems where the constraint isn't time or budget but conceptual — what is this thing, even? Working alongside one or two people who think out loud well. Coaching a designer through the moment their work clicks. Drains me: meetings that should have been async, politics dressed up as strategy, and shipping something I know is wrong because the calendar says so."

Self-aware, specific, no fake humility. Hiring managers actually use this answer to gauge fit.

## B. Design Philosophy & Process (9–18)

### 9. What's your design philosophy?
Two or three principles you actually live by, each backed by a real decision:
> "Three principles. **Clarity over cleverness** — if the interface needs explanation, the design isn't done. At [company] I killed an animated onboarding because it scored higher on delight tests but lower on task completion. **The user's hardest moment is your design brief** — most teams design the happy path; I design the error state first and work backward. **Make the reasoning visible** — both in product (showing why an AI suggested something) and in team (showing why I made the call I did)."

Avoid generic principles. "User-centric" is not a philosophy.

### 10. How do you define great design vs. good design?
> "Good design solves the stated problem. Great design solves the stated problem and reframes it so the next team builds on something stronger. Good design ships and the metric moves. Great design ships and the team's vocabulary changes — six months later you hear PMs and engineers using language that came from a design call. That's the highest bar I know."

This answer signals senior-level systems thinking.

### 11. Walk me through your end-to-end design process.
Don't recite a textbook. Walk through *your* specific approach:
> "I run a loop, not a waterfall. Phase 1 is framing — I'd rather spend an extra week on what the problem actually is than a month on the wrong one. Phase 2 is parallel exploration — I always sketch three radically different directions before going deep on one; this prevents converging too early. Phase 3 is critique — internal first, then with PM and engineering, then with users. Phase 4 is high-fidelity + prototype, often in V0 or Claude Code at this point because the prototype IS the spec. Phase 5 is ship + measure. Phase 6 is the post-mortem — non-negotiable."

Highlight the phase that competitors skip. For Botpress, that's framing.

### 12. How do you handle ambiguity?
> "I get more comfortable as a problem gets vaguer — that's where the leverage is. My move: write the brief myself, in one paragraph, then circulate it. Half the time the team disagrees with my framing, and that's the most productive disagreement we can have. The worst thing you can do with ambiguity is wait for someone to clarify it for you."

This question is testing seniority. Demonstrate you frame, not receive.

### 13. How do you balance speed and quality?
> "False binary. The fastest path to quality is killing scope early — the cost of a bad design isn't the design, it's the engineering and the cleanup. My rule: at week one, what could we ship that solves 70% of the problem at 100% craft? Not the other way around. At Botpress especially, the velocity question is mostly a scope question wearing a speed costume."

Lean startups love this answer.

### 14. How do you know when a design is "done"?
> "Three signals. One — the design defends itself in critique without me explaining it. Two — the engineering questions stop being 'what does this mean' and start being 'how do we implement.' Three — when I look at it a week later I want to ship it, not redo it. If any of the three are missing, it's not done."

Concrete, defendable, unique to you.

### 15. What's the role of research in your process?
> "Research is a forcing function for honest framing. I don't do research to validate — I do it to find out where I'm wrong. The cadence I run: lightweight customer calls weekly (3–5), structured studies quarterly, dashboards always on. At Botpress, the unique research challenge is that there are two users — the agent builder and the agent's end customer. I'd want to know early how you weight those two."

This pivots to a smart question back.

### 16. How do you incorporate user feedback?
> "Triage. Three buckets: 'this is a symptom of a deeper problem,' 'this is a real new requirement,' 'this is one user's preference.' The first bucket gets a research follow-up. The second goes to the roadmap. The third gets archived with thanks. The mistake junior teams make is treating all three the same and shipping a Frankenstein product."

### 17. How do you balance data and intuition?
> "Data tells you what happened. Intuition tells you what to test next. I trust quantitative data for *measurement*, qualitative for *direction*, and intuition for *hypothesis*. Anyone who claims they don't use intuition is just hiding it. The discipline is making your intuition falsifiable — if I think X will work, I name the metric that would prove me wrong."

### 18. What's your stance on design trends?
> "Mostly skeptical. A trend that survives 5 years becomes a pattern. A trend that doesn't was probably solving someone's portfolio, not their user. The trends I'm watching seriously right now: streaming UIs, agent permission patterns, and ambient AI (interfaces where the AI runs without a chat box). The trends I ignore: anything that requires me to use the word 'experiential.'"

Self-aware, opinionated, ages you up.

## C. Player-Coach: Managing Juniors While Shipping (19–33)

### 19. How do you balance hands-on IC work with managing juniors?
**The most important answer in this entire doc.** Memorize it.
> "Default 55/25/20: ship/coach/strategy. The IC percentage stays high specifically because juniors learn by watching me do the work, agent UX is too new to hand off, and a lean startup can't afford a manager who codes nothing. The ratio shifts toward coaching as juniors level up and after I hire a mid-level — which is my second hire, not my fifth. The trap I watch for is IC drift, where I take the fun ambiguous work and leave juniors with cleanup. The fix is a written rule: if I'm picking up a piece of work, I ask whether a junior could grow from it. If yes, I pair instead of take."

Specific numbers + named trap + named correction = credible.

### 20. How do you decide which work to take vs. delegate?
> "Four-cell matrix. I take: ambiguous 0→1 problems with no precedent, and work that sets a pattern the team will reuse. I delegate: well-scoped iterations on known surfaces, and work where 'good enough + ship' beats 'perfect + late.' The trick is that hard work doesn't always go to me. A junior taking on a known surface end-to-end is harder for them than ambiguous work, and that's exactly the kind of stretch they need."

### 21. How do you onboard a new junior designer?
> "First 30 days: shadow me on one project, get assigned one well-scoped piece of their own, get exposed to every cross-functional partner. By day 30 they should have shipped something small with their name on it. First 60: they own a feature end-to-end with me in critique twice a week. First 90: they've presented their work in a cross-functional meeting and survived. The trap to avoid: making them comfortable so long they never feel the productive discomfort of ownership."

### 22. Tell me about a junior you helped grow.
*Substitute your real story.* Structure:
> "*[Name]* came in as [level], strong on craft, weak on defending their work in critique. They'd ship something and fold the moment someone pushed back. The intervention I ran: every week for two months they had to bring one piece to me with three written defenses against the most likely objections — before showing me the design. By month three they were running critique sessions themselves. They're now [level/role]."

Specific person, specific gap, specific intervention, specific outcome.

### 23. Tell me about a junior who didn't work out.
The dangerous question. Answer it honestly, owning your role:
> "*[Name]* came in with strong portfolio but couldn't translate critique into action. Over six months I tried [intervention 1], [intervention 2], and [intervention 3]. The pattern persisted. The mistake I made was holding the bar steady too long out of guilt — I should have had the explicit conversation at month two, not month five. We parted ways at month seven. What I learned: feedback that doesn't change behavior in 30 days is feedback the person doesn't have the tools to act on. Either you teach the tool or you call it."

Never blame the junior. Own the timing.

### 24. A junior is stuck on a design at 9pm before ship. What do you do?
> "It depends on stakes, and that's the answer. Low stakes — let them ship the B-grade version and we fix it in the morning; the lesson is more valuable than the polish. High stakes — pair with them for 30 minutes, not silently take over. If I take over silently, two things happen: they don't learn, and they don't trust me next time. The one thing I never do is take it over without telling them. The debrief the next morning is non-negotiable."

This is a trick question testing for the bottleneck pattern. Naming the trap shows seniority.

### 25. How do you avoid becoming the bottleneck?
Five tactics, memorized:
> "One — office hours twice a week, so juniors batch questions instead of interrupting. Two — written 'default direction' memos for ambiguous areas, so juniors decide without me until they hit something explicitly listed. Three — make my reasoning visible via short Looms or written rationales on my own work; juniors see how I think, not just what I decided. Four — explicit promotion targets per junior every six months. Five — hire a mid-level fast. The second hire is the leverage hire, not the third or fourth."

### 26. How do you teach taste without dictating taste?
> "Taste is the accumulated record of a thousand small decisions. You can't transfer it in a sentence — you transfer it by exposing your reasoning on each one. My move: when I make a call in critique, I name the alternative I rejected and why. Not 'do it this way' — 'I'd consider both X and Y, here's the tradeoff, here's where I'd land and why.' Over months, the junior absorbs the tradeoff vocabulary, not the conclusion. Eventually they make different calls than me, and that's the goal."

### 27. How do you set growth goals for juniors?
> "One per six months, written, dated. Not 'get better at X' — measurable: 'lead one feature end-to-end including stakeholder review by Q3.' I co-write it with the designer; they sign it. We review monthly. The mistake is having 14 goals — by month two you remember none of them. One clear goal beats five vague ones."

### 28. How do you decide when a junior is ready for promotion?
> "A leveling rubric they can see — no surprises. The signal isn't time-served, it's three things in evidence: they can scope their own work without me, they can defend it in critique without me, and they can mentor someone newer. Until all three are true, they're not ready. I'd rather promote late and have it stick than early and have to walk it back."

### 29. A junior repeatedly misses the bar on craft. Walk me through 90 days.
> "Week one — specific, written feedback with examples. Not 'tighten craft' — 'compare the spacing here vs. how Mike did it in [project]; this is what I mean.' Week three — written improvement plan with three observable behaviors and a check-in cadence. Week eight — honest reassessment. If trajectory is positive, we keep going. If not, week eight is when we have the harder conversation. Week 12 — decision. I document everything. The kindest thing you can do for someone struggling is be explicit early. The cruel thing is the silent month."

### 30. How do you give a stretch project without setting them up to fail?
> "Scaffolding. The project itself can be ambitious — the scaffolding is what de-risks it. Three things I put around any stretch: a clearer-than-usual brief from me, a more senior partner (often me) available for 30 min twice a week, and a defined 'minimum success' so they know what shipping the B-grade version looks like. The goal is the discomfort of ownership, not the panic of free-fall."

### 31. How do you do hands-on work in a way that scales your team?
> "Two ways. First, I pick the IC work that sets patterns the team will reuse — a navigation pattern, a critique-able prototype, a design-system component. My IC output becomes leverage for the next ten designs. Second, I make my reasoning visible — Looms, written rationales, even rough sketches with annotations. The point isn't that I shipped a beautiful screen, it's that the next time a junior faces a similar problem, they have a reference."

### 32. How do you handle a junior who disagrees with your direction?
> "Welcome it, then make them work for it. The phrase I use: 'Convince me. Walk me through the alternative and where it's stronger than what I proposed.' Half the time they change my mind, and I tell them so explicitly — that's the most important moment, because it shows the disagreement was real. The other half, they realize their case is thinner than they thought, and that's also a learning. The version that doesn't work: silent disagreement followed by quiet compliance. That's how good designers leave."

### 33. How do you protect juniors from stakeholder politics?
> "Selectively. Some shielding — yes, I take the political hits on naming, on roadmap fights, on the moment a VP overrules a design decision. But not all shielding. Juniors need exposure to stakeholders to grow. The rule I use: shield from the political dynamics, but bring them into the substantive disagreements. They need to learn to defend their work in a room where people don't already agree with them."

## D. Critique & Feedback (34–41)

### 34. How do you run design critique?
The JD calls this out by name. Have the rehearsed answer:
> "Weekly, 60 minutes, max four designers, predictable cadence. Format: each designer brings one piece in progress, 5 minutes of context, 15 minutes of feedback. I go last so I don't anchor the room. Rules I enforce: feedback addresses the work not the person, alternatives must be specific not 'I'd just…,' and the designer owns whether to adopt the feedback. After critique, I document themes — patterns that come up across sessions tell us what to teach next. Once a quarter we do a critique of critique itself — what's working in the process, what's not."

Specific format + named rules + a meta-loop = senior-grade answer.

### 35. How do you give constructive feedback?
> "Three layers. The fact — what I see. The interpretation — why I think it matters. The ask — what could change. Most feedback fails because people skip layers. 'This is confusing' is useless. 'The CTA on screen 3 sits below the fold on mobile, which means users on the most common viewport miss it; consider promoting it above the input or changing the input hierarchy' — that's a critique someone can act on."

### 36. How do you receive critical feedback?
> "Curiosity, not defense. The instinct everyone has is to explain — 'oh I tried that and it didn't work because…' I've trained myself to start with 'tell me more.' Half the time the person's pointing at something real I hadn't seen. The other half, the act of explaining further reveals their concern is actually different from how they phrased it. Defending too fast costs you both signals."

### 37. How do you handle a designer who's defensive about feedback?
> "Two moves. One — model receiving feedback well myself in front of them. Two — call it out, kindly, in the moment: 'I notice when I bring up [X] the answer often starts with explanation. Can we try the version where you just sit with it for 30 seconds first?' Defensiveness is almost always a fear response — usually fear of being seen as a junior. The work is making it safe to be unfinished."

### 38. What's the difference between critique and direction?
> "Critique surfaces options and questions. Direction makes a call. Both have a place. Critique is what you do in week one of a project; direction is what you do at 4pm on Friday when ship is Monday. The mistake leaders make is using direction when critique is still possible — you shortcut the team's learning. The opposite mistake is using critique when direction is needed — you waste the team's time."

### 39. How do you maintain quality across designers with different skill levels?
> "Two mechanisms. One — written principles and patterns, so quality isn't subjective. Two — critique that levels up the floor faster than the ceiling. You raise the team by improving the weakest piece in the room, not by celebrating the strongest. And the most senior designer (me, at Botpress) ships work too, so the bar is visible — not just talked about."

### 40. How do you teach the language of critique?
> "Vocabulary, then practice. Vocabulary: hierarchy, affordance, mode, latency, error state, trust signal — the words that let people point at things precisely. Practice: assign a designer to lead critique on someone else's piece. They have to come prepared with three concrete observations. The first few times it's awkward. By session five they're better at critique than 80% of people I've worked with."

### 41. How do you handle conflicting feedback in critique?
> "Surface it explicitly. 'Sam thinks the CTA should be primary; Alex thinks it should be secondary; here's what I think and why, and here's what the designer should decide.' The worst version is letting the designer hold three conflicting opinions and figure it out alone — they'll either freeze or pick one and waste effort defending it. The team needs to model that disagreement is the point of critique, and resolution is the designer's job."

## E. Hiring & Team Building (42–49)

### 42. How would you build out the design team in 12 months?
> "Depends on roadmap, but assuming AI-agent surface is the bet — my hiring sequence: first, a mid-level designer to take coaching pressure off me and ship alongside the juniors. Second, a content/conversation designer — this is the most under-hired role at agent companies. Third, another junior or junior-to-mid. I'd avoid hiring another senior IC early; the structure becomes a peer hierarchy without me having the room to operate. Total target: four to six designers including me by month 12, with a content designer in the mix."

Concrete sequence, specific rationale.

### 43. What's your hiring philosophy?
> "Hire for slope, not intercept. A designer who's slightly weaker today but learning visibly fast beats a stronger designer who's plateaued. The other rule: hire for the team you'll have, not the team you have. The mid-level I hire today should be capable of being the senior I need in 18 months."

### 44. What do you look for in a junior designer?
> "Three things, in order. Taste — they can tell good from bad and articulate why. Hunger — they critique their own work harder than I would. Trajectory — their portfolio from a year ago is visibly worse than today. I care less about which tools they know, what school they went to, or how many companies they've worked at."

### 45. What do you look for in a mid-level or senior?
> "Ownership and clarity. A mid-level should be able to scope, ship, and defend a piece of work without me as the safety net. A senior should be able to mentor below them and partner with engineering and PM as equals. The signal I look for: in the portfolio review, do they talk about their team and their stakeholders — or only their pixels?"

### 46. How do you assess design taste in an interview?
> "Two exercises. One — pick three apps you use; what's working, what's not, what would you change. This tests articulation more than taste, but they correlate. Two — show them a piece of mediocre work and ask what they'd change. Mediocre work is the test — bad work is too easy, great work invites flattery. If they tell me a polished mediocre design is great, that's the answer."

### 47. How do you decide between two strong candidates?
> "Reference checks and a coffee. References tell me how they behave when no one's watching. The coffee — informal conversation about something other than design — tells me whether the team will want to work alongside this person on a Tuesday at 4pm. Both signals matter as much as the portfolio."

### 48. What's your interview process?
> "Five steps, six weeks max. Recruiter screen for fit. 45-minute conversation with me — story, philosophy, behavior. Portfolio review, 90 minutes — two case studies deep. Cross-functional panel with PM and engineering. Final conversation with the founder. I send the rubric ahead of time. The whole process should respect the candidate's time as much as it tests it — long opaque processes lose the best people."

### 49. How do you know when you've made a bad hire?
> "Two signals, both within 60 days. One — they can't tell me what they're stuck on. Two — feedback doesn't change behavior. The first means they don't have visibility into their own work, the second means they don't have the tools to act on input. If both show up in the first 60 days and don't move in another 30, it's a bad hire. The cost of acting fast is small; the cost of holding on out of guilt is enormous to the team."

## F. Cross-Functional Partnership (50–59)

### 50. How do you partner with engineering?
> "Pair early, prototype together. The handoff document is a lie — engineering should see the design at the sketch stage, not the redline stage. At Botpress specifically, with Claude Code and V0 in the prototyping flow, my hope is that design and engineering converge on the prototype-as-spec model. I sit in eng standups when relevant, I learn enough of the system to know what I'm asking for, and I never argue with an engineer about whether something is 'easy' — I ask what makes it hard."

### 51. How do you partner with product managers?
> "Co-author, don't receive. The healthy version: PM and I write the brief together, sometimes in the same doc. The unhealthy version: PM hands me a spec and I push pixels. At Botpress, I'd want a tight loop with my PM partner — weekly 1:1, shared roadmap doc, shared metrics. If the PM and I aren't in conflict over priorities at least once a quarter, one of us isn't pushing hard enough."

### 52. Tell me about a time you disagreed with engineering.
*Substitute your real story.* Structure:
> "At [company], we were shipping [feature]. Engineering said the interaction I'd designed required [N] weeks because of [reason]. I disagreed — I thought there was a simpler implementation path. I asked them to walk me through it, and at the point where I'd have lost a non-technical argument, I learned why my path was actually harder. We landed on a third option neither of us had proposed. The lesson: 'disagreement' often means 'incomplete information shared.'"

### 53. Tell me about a time you disagreed with a PM.
> "We were prioritizing [project A] vs. [project B]. PM said A. I argued B because [reason — usually: more strategic, more user-aligned, sets a pattern]. We ran a one-week investigation — I put together a doc with three pieces of evidence, the PM put together their case. We brought it to engineering and the founder. Decision went to B. The thing I learned: don't try to win in a 1:1. Build the case, surface it to the broader team, let the decision be transparent."

### 54. How do you handle when engineering pushes back on a design as "too expensive"?
> "First reaction: thank them and ask what makes it expensive. Often the expensive part is a sub-feature I don't care that much about, and we can ship the simpler version. Sometimes the expensive part IS the design point, and I need to argue for it with the cost in mind. The version I avoid: insisting on the original design without engaging the cost. That's how design loses credibility for the next ten conversations."

### 55. How do you co-write specs with PMs?
> "The doc has three sections written together: problem, constraints, success criteria. After that, PM owns scope and timeline, I own interaction and craft, we both own user flow. We share the doc with engineering on day one, not day 30. The model I want to avoid is the spec-as-handoff — that's where design and product diverge silently and discover the divergence too late."

### 56. How do you involve engineers early in design?
> "Sketches in week one. I bring engineers into the framing conversation before pixels — they often spot constraints that change the design direction entirely. The rule: an engineer should be the third pair of eyes on a design, not the tenth. They feel ownership, the design ships smoother, and you avoid the 'engineering keeps changing the design' complaint."

### 57. How do you handle a stakeholder who keeps changing requirements?
> "Find the root. Either the underlying problem isn't well-defined, or the stakeholder is responding to fear of being wrong, or something has changed in the business. I have a different response to each. For undefined problem — pause and re-scope. For fear — give them a low-stakes way to weigh in earlier. For business change — surface it explicitly so the whole team adjusts, not just design."

### 58. How do you build trust with a skeptical engineering team?
> "Two moves. Ship something they didn't expect a designer to ship — a working prototype, a code-adjacent contribution, a clear spec they didn't have to translate. Two — admit when I don't know. Engineers smell BS, and they reward designers who say 'I don't know how that's implemented; teach me.' Trust comes from demonstrated competence + visible humility."

### 59. What do you expect from your PM partner?
> "Clarity on what we're not building, not just what we are. Honesty about ambiguity — if they don't know yet, I want to hear that, not a manufactured spec. Defending design when I'm not in the room. And the hard one: pushing back on me when I'm wrong, in private, before it becomes a public misalignment."

## G. AI / Agent UX Expertise (60–74)

### 60. What's your view on AI / agent UX right now?
**The keystone answer.** Five sentences:
> "We're at the moment where the medium is wider than the language to describe it. The patterns that will define the next decade are being set in the next 18 months by a small number of teams. The unsolved problems I care most about: how do you communicate confidence and uncertainty without paralyzing the user; how do you design tool-use confirmation that's neither too noisy nor too permissive; and how do you build memory that feels useful without feeling surveilled. Most products today solve one of those three and ignore the other two. The opportunity at Botpress is to build the design system that lets thousands of teams build agents without re-solving these problems from scratch."

### 61. How do you design for streaming responses?
> "Streaming changes the contract — the user knows generation is happening, so the interface should make the latency feel like progress, not a freeze. Practical rules I use: show tokens as they arrive even if you stop and re-render later; surface a 'still working' indicator at 8 seconds; allow interruption with a clear 'stop' affordance; never show a static spinner where streaming is possible. The mistake teams make is treating streaming as the same UX problem as a loading state. It isn't — streaming IS the UI."

### 62. How do you handle hallucinations in the UI?
> "Layered disclosure. Three tiers — confident with source, confident without source, low-confidence with caveat. Different visual treatments for each. For a builder tool like Botpress, the additional layer is teaching the *builder* to design their agent's hallucination behavior — when to fall back, when to escalate, when to refuse. The UX for managing hallucinations starts at agent design time, not run time."

### 63. Design a tool-use confirmation flow for an agent.
> "Three modes, user-selectable per tool type. Mode one — 'auto-run with audit log,' for low-stakes reversible actions. Mode two — 'confirm before run,' the default for new tools, with a clear summary of what the action will do and what it will affect. Mode three — 'require justification,' for high-stakes irreversible actions like sending money or deleting data. The key insight: confirmation fatigue is real. If every action confirms, none of them mean anything. Tiering is the design."

### 64. How do you communicate confidence and uncertainty?
> "Three signals. Language — 'I think,' 'I'm fairly sure,' 'I don't know' carry different meaning. Visual — a confidence bar or color is the wrong default in most cases because it implies precision the model doesn't have. Source — showing what the answer is grounded in does more for trust than a confidence score ever does. Best designs combine language and source; few designs need a visual confidence indicator at all."

### 65. How do you design for non-deterministic outputs?
> "Treat the surface as a contract, not a layout. The user should know what categories of response they'll get even if the specific content varies. Practical move: design a 'frame' that wraps any output — a header with source, a body with the response, a footer with feedback affordances. The frame is deterministic, the content isn't. This is how you build trust over thousands of varied interactions."

### 66. How do you handle long conversations exceeding context windows?
> "Three layers. Summarization that the user can see and edit — they should know what the agent is remembering and what it's forgetting. Pinned messages for things the user explicitly says 'remember this.' Graceful degradation when context truncates — surface it, don't hide it. The worst pattern is silent forgetting; users lose trust when the agent contradicts itself and they don't understand why."

### 67. What's your stance on suggested replies vs. open input?
> "Both, layered. Suggested replies for the 80% of common intents — they teach users what's possible and shortcut the typing. Open input for the 20% where suggestions don't fit, but it has to be obviously available, not hidden. The mistake is forcing all users into one or the other. Power users want open; new users want suggestions. The interface should know which one is looking."

### 68. How would you add memory to a conversational UX?
> "Visible by default. Three principles. One — the user can see what's remembered, at any time, in one click. Two — the user can edit or delete memories. Three — memory is opt-in for sensitive categories. The bar is set by the worst surveillance product the user has used before yours — your job is to clear that bar so obviously they trust you within a session."

### 69. How do you design observability into an agent builder?
> "Three lenses. Build-time — what happens when the builder is testing? Replay, eval, A/B. Runtime — what happens when an end user hits an edge case? Logs, traces, escalation. Diagnostic — when something's wrong, can the builder find it? This is where most agent platforms fail today; they make it easy to build the happy path and impossible to debug the unhappy one."

### 70. How do you design for multi-step agent failures?
> "Two questions: where did it fail, and can it recover. The UI needs to show the steps, mark the failure, and offer three options — retry, edit the step, or abandon. The mistake teams make is showing a generic 'something went wrong.' For multi-step agents, that's useless. The user is investing in a flow; the interface owes them the specifics of which step broke."

### 71. What patterns work for visual flow editors?
> "Three observations from looking at Botpress, Voiceflow, n8n, and Zapier. One — vertical flow scales better than horizontal for complex agents; horizontal looks pretty in demos and breaks at scale. Two — nodes should have a 'collapsed' state by default with progressive disclosure on hover; full detail is overwhelming. Three — the canvas needs a structured outline view as an alternative to the visual graph, because past a certain complexity the graph itself becomes unreadable. The best builders ship both."

### 72. How do you balance no-code simplicity with developer power?
> "Layered abstraction, not separate products. The novice sees the visual graph. The intermediate sees inline code blocks. The expert sees the full code escape hatch and can drop into raw config. Each layer should be discoverable from the one below. The anti-pattern is the 'simple mode' and 'advanced mode' toggle — users get stuck at the wrong layer because moving between them feels like starting over."

### 73. What's the biggest UX problem with current AI products?
> "Trust calibration. Most AI products are either too confident — answer everything in the same authoritative tone — or too hedged — caveat everything until the answer is useless. The skill is variable trust signals tied to actual model confidence. Almost no product does this well today. Whoever cracks the design pattern wins a lot."

### 74. How do you stay current on AI UX?
> "Three inputs. One — I use the products I'm competing with weekly, not as a designer but as a user trying to get something done. Two — I read the research from Anthropic, OpenAI, and the safety community; the design implications are buried in there. Three — I rebuild patterns I see. The fastest way to understand a new interaction is to prototype it yourself in Claude Code or V0."

## H. Design Systems for AI (75–82)

### 75. How do you extend a design system to handle AI patterns?
> "Three additions on top of a traditional system. One — components for streaming and progressive disclosure: skeleton states, partial-content cards, interruption affordances. Two — confidence/source patterns: citation chips, hedge language tokens, fallback states. Three — agent control surfaces: tool permission cards, memory viewers, conversation thread management. The hard part isn't building the components, it's the principles that govern when to use which one."

### 76. What new components are needed for AI-first products?
> "At minimum: a streaming text container with stop affordance, a tool-call card with collapse/expand and audit log, a citation/source chip, a memory pill, a confidence indicator (used sparingly), an interruption banner, and an agent-state indicator (thinking, calling tool, waiting on user). Most existing systems have zero of these. Botpress's design system needs all seven within 12 months."

### 77. How do you handle the non-deterministic UI problem?
> "Frame and contract. The frame is deterministic — same layout, same hierarchy, same affordances. The contract is what kinds of content can appear in the frame. The content itself varies. Users build trust on the frame and the contract; they don't need the content to be the same. Designing the frame well is half the job in AI UX."

### 78. When do you build vs. buy design system tooling?
> "Buy for the 80% — Figma libraries, token tools, documentation platforms. Build for the 20% that's specific to your product — at Botpress that's anything related to the agent builder canvas itself. The rule: if a tool exists and works, buying it costs less than the engineering cycles to build worse. The exception is when the tool is so central to your product that owning it is strategic."

### 79. How do you make a design system that juniors can extend safely?
> "Guardrails, not gates. Three layers. Layer one — primitive tokens (color, type, space) — locked, junior can use but not change. Layer two — components — junior can extend with approval. Layer three — patterns — junior can propose and we discuss in critique. The mistake is locking everything, which means juniors work around the system; or locking nothing, which means inconsistency."

### 80. How do you measure design system success?
> "Three metrics. Adoption — what % of new features ship using system components? Coverage — what % of common patterns are in the system? Velocity — how long does it take to ship a new feature now vs. before the system? The vanity metric to avoid is component count; more components isn't better, more reuse is."

### 81. How do you handle design system debt?
> "Quarterly audit. Identify what's stale, what's underused, what's been duplicated. Sunset components with documented migration paths — never just delete. The cardinal sin is letting two components do the same thing because deprecating the old one was 'too disruptive.' Pick one. Migrate. Move on."

### 82. What's the relationship between design system and brand?
> "Brand sets the principles; system delivers them. Brand says 'we feel approachable and precise'; system answers with the actual color, the actual letter-spacing, the actual interaction speed. Brand without system is poetry without execution. System without brand is execution without point of view. The Head of UX owns the bridge between them."

## I. Botpress-Specific (83–90)

### 83. Critique Botpress Studio. What would you change in your first 30 days?
**Build it yourself before the interview. Walk in with this answer in detail.**
Skeleton:
> "I spent [N] hours building [the agent you built]. Three specific friction points. [Friction one — be specific about screen, click, decision]. [Friction two — same level of specificity]. [Friction three — same]. Each one violates a different principle: [principle one] — clarity over cleverness; [principle two] — the user's hardest moment is your design brief; [principle three] — make the reasoning visible. In my first 30 days I'd fix one of these end-to-end as a visible IC ship, and run critique with the team on the other two so we can collectively decide the pattern."

The candidate who actually used the product wins this question. Almost no one does.

### 84. How would you improve our onboarding for non-developers?
> "First, identify the moment of magic — the point where a non-developer feels 'oh, I can do this.' For most agent builders that's the first response from the agent that they recognize as something they shaped. Compress the path to that moment. Today, [if you built it] it took [X] minutes; my target is under [Y]. The cuts come from removing decisions, not adding hand-holding. Defaults the user can change later beat questions the user has to answer now."

### 85. What's our biggest differentiator vs. Voiceflow / Copilot Studio?
> "From my outside view: Botpress's bet on being both developer-extensible AND non-developer-accessible is rarer than the market gives credit for. Voiceflow leans non-developer; Rasa and Copilot Studio lean developer. The Botpress bet only works if the design holds both audiences without compromising either, and that's a hard design problem. If that hypothesis is right, it's a moat. If it's wrong, the product splits into two products. I'd want to understand which version is on the roadmap."

Probe + pivot to a smart question back.

### 86. How would you redesign the visual flow editor?
*Tailor based on what you actually built.*
> "Three changes. One — collapse node detail by default; expand on hover or click; reduce visual noise. Two — add a parallel outline view that mirrors the canvas; once flows get complex, the graph becomes unreadable and an outline is the better mental model. Three — make the failure path as visible as the happy path; in agent building, debugging is half the work, and most tools hide it."

### 87. Design the agent-testing experience.
> "Three modes layered into one surface. One — quick test, free-form chat, immediate feedback. Two — scenario replay, where the builder runs through a set of saved conversations and sees outputs side by side for comparison across versions. Three — eval mode, where the builder defines pass/fail conditions and gets pass-rate metrics on a corpus. Most builders have one. The full set is what enterprise customers actually need."

### 88. How would you onboard a new enterprise customer?
> "Two tracks. Track one — the design experience: a guided first agent that ships in under an hour, using the customer's actual content. Track two — the trust experience: clear documentation on security, model choice, data handling, observability. Enterprise customers don't choose tools on UX alone; they choose on the combination of 'does this work' and 'can I defend this to my CISO.' Both tracks ship together."

### 89. What's the next big feature you'd push for?
> "Based on outside view — agent eval and observability tooling. The market has moved from 'can we build agents' to 'are our agents working,' and the tools for the second question are weak across the board. Building this in-product, rather than ceding it to LangSmith / Langfuse, would be a strong move. Open to learning what your roadmap already has here."

### 90. What concerns you about our product strategy?
A risky but powerful question to answer well:
> "Two concerns based on what I can see from outside. One — the dual-audience bet on developers and non-developers is hard to hold; I'd want to understand how you're navigating the natural pull toward one or the other. Two — agent platforms are commoditizing on the build side; the differentiation will increasingly be in the design system that makes building specific kinds of agents fast, and in the ecosystem. Both are design-led problems and I'd want to know how you weight them."

Honest, specific, design-flavored. Don't sycophant — this is the question that separates senior candidates.

## J. Stakeholder / Leadership / Situational (91–100)

### 91. How do you handle a disagreement with the founder or CEO?
> "Disagree in private, commit in public, document the disagreement. The version I avoid: arguing in front of the team — it signals dysfunction. The other version I avoid: silent compliance — that's how design loses voice over time. The middle path: make my case once, hard, in private. If I lose, I commit and execute. If the outcome proves me right later, I bring it back as a learning, not a 'told you so.' Founders remember both."

### 92. Tell me about a project that failed.
*Substitute your real story. Don't pick a safe failure.*
> "At [company] I shipped [project]. It missed [specific metric] by [specific amount]. The root cause: I anchored on [framing] when the actual problem was [different framing]. I learned about it from [user research / data / customer complaint], two months too late. What I'd do differently — I'd have run a wider framing exercise before going deep, and I'd have set a kill criterion at week four. The lesson changed how I run projects: every new framing gets a written one-paragraph problem statement that the team has to agree on before we sketch."

Specific, owned, with a behavior change.

### 93. Tell me about a time you had to pivot mid-project.
> "[Specific project] was [X] weeks in when [new information] surfaced. We had three options: hold the course and miss the bigger insight, scrap and restart, or fold the new insight into the existing direction. I picked option three — kept the framing, redesigned the core interaction. The cost was two weeks of throwaway work and one tense meeting with my PM. The benefit was a stronger ship and a team that learned the pivot was a feature, not a failure. I'd rather pivot honestly than ship the wrong thing on time."

### 94. Tell me about a time you said no to a stakeholder.
> "[Specific stakeholder] requested [feature]. I said no because [reason — strategic misalignment / poor scoping / better alternative]. I didn't say no flat — I said 'here's why I think this doesn't ship; here's the alternative I think serves your underlying goal.' Half the time, the alternative landed. The other half they overruled me, and we shipped it; some of those decisions I still think were wrong, and one or two changed my mind. The point isn't always winning — it's making sure no isn't a default."

### 95. How do you measure design success?
> "Three layers. Product metrics — task success, retention, conversion, NPS. Process metrics — velocity, design debt, critique participation. People metrics — junior growth trajectory, team retention, hiring quality. Most teams measure only the first layer; senior design leaders are accountable for all three. At Botpress, layer one would include adoption of agent features, time-to-first-agent, and builder-task completion rates."

### 96. How do you advocate for UX with leadership?
> "Tie design to outcomes leadership already cares about. Don't fight for 'good design'; fight for 'design that moves the metric you're measured on.' At the board level, the only number that matters is the revenue story; my job is to translate design decisions into revenue language. Internally, I do the opposite — translate revenue pressure into design constraints. The skill is being bilingual."

### 97. What does your first 30-60-90 look like?
**Memorize this one — high-value at the final round.**
> "Days 0-30 — Listen and ship one small thing. 1:1s with every designer in depth, every PM and engineering lead, the founder. Twenty customer calls. Audit the design system, research repo, critique cadence. Ship one small piece of IC work myself — proves the craft, gives juniors a model.
>
> Days 31-60 — Diagnose and frame. Share back three problems and three strengths. Stand up weekly critique with a documented format. Identify one quick win to ship — led by a junior with me as coach, not solo by me. Define how design success will be measured.
>
> Days 61-90 — Commit. Ship the junior-led quick win. Propose the 12-month roadmap for design ops and the design system. Hire a mid-level — the leverage hire. Queue stretch projects for each junior with explicit growth goals.
>
> Caveat: this is a hypothesis. The first 30 days will rewrite it — that's the point."

### 98. How do you handle compressing a timeline without sacrificing quality?
> "Kill scope, not craft. The trade is almost never 'ship lower quality faster' — it's 'ship less, at full quality, faster.' My move: at week one of a compressed project, what's the 70% of the problem we can solve at 100% craft? Ship that. The remaining 30% goes to a v2. Engineering and PM love this framing because it gives them a clean cut, not a degraded experience."

### 99. Describe a design crisis you handled.
> "[Specific moment — launch eve issue, broken flow at scale, etc.]. The pattern that helped: separating decisions that had to happen in the next 4 hours from decisions that could happen in the next week. We made the 4-hour decisions with limited information and accepted some debt. We made the week-long decisions with full team input. The crisis was real; the panic was optional. Calm, structured triage is the only design move in a crisis."

### 100. What questions do you have for us?
**The closer. Most candidates blow it. Senior leaders use it to test fit.**
Three of these, customized to who you're talking to:
> "1. What does design success look like 12 months from now — and what's the most likely reason it doesn't happen?
> 2. The role description leans both leadership and IC. What does the actual day-to-day split look like for the person in this seat?
> 3. Of the designers currently on the team, who's the one I'd be working hardest to grow in my first six months?
> 4. What's the biggest disagreement you've had with the founder about product direction, and how did it resolve?
> 5. What's the part of Botpress you're most worried about right now?
> 6. If I'm sitting here a year from now and the company is celebrating, what specifically happened?
> 7. What's the most surprising thing about working here that someone outside wouldn't guess?"

Ask the same question across hiring manager, peers, and a junior. Listen for inconsistencies. That's the most valuable signal you'll get.
