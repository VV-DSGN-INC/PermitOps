This knowledge base supplies the missing spine — the **company-first mindset** — and a vocabulary of named frameworks that signal you operate at the level the title implies. It also forces a harder strategic question about whether "Head of UX at Botpress" is even the right move, and gives you the tools to decode it.

## 1. Decode "Head of UX" BEFORE you rehearse a single answer

The knowledge base's `career-stages.md` is blunt about title chaos. Three facts that directly hit your situation:

1. **"Lead/Head" is regionally ambiguous.** 65% of EMEA "lead" roles are people-management; US "lead" skews IC. Botpress is Canadian — convention is unsettled. **You cannot prep correctly until you know which one this is.**
2. **Title gap by company size.** A "Head of UX" at a ~Series-A startup is functionally a **senior/staff IC + small-team lead**, NOT a director. This is good news: your 10+ years and founding-designer DNA (Acies.ai, MeetnGreetMe) map *exactly* to "most experienced designer in the room who also grows juniors." You're not under-qualified; you're calibrated.
3. **"Staff/Head roles are only real at two company types"**: Big Tech/late-stage, OR design-led startups (Linear, Notion, Mercury — design-led culture, high design maturity, design-background founders). **Botpress must be category 2 for this role to be real.** If it's not, "Head of UX" is a title without budget, headcount, or a leveling doc behind it.

**Action — ask these in the recruiter screen, not the final round:**
- "Is there a design leveling doc / competency matrix I can see?"
- "How many designers are on the team today, and at what levels?"
- "Is this role hands-on IC with mentoring, or primarily people-management?"
- "Who owns design decisions today, and who would I report to?"

Run **Lara Hogan's "Identifying Your Role"** (from the workbook) before the offer: write four lists — Must-haves, Nice-to-haves, Don't-cares, and the one-line "I'm optimizing for." Your bio says you're optimizing for *"a permanent seat on a real design team, not another solo-contractor role."* If Botpress's "Head of UX" is a one-designer-plus-juniors shop, that's structurally close to the solo role you said you're done with. **Decide this before you fall in love with the offer.**

## 2. The spine: company-first mindset rewires every answer

This is the knowledge base's #1 recurring thread and the single biggest change to your prep.

**The diagram:** designers see `MY WORK` as the big circle containing `BUSINESS`. Everyone who approves budgets — founders, PMs, eng leads — sees `BUSINESS` as the big circle containing `MY WORK`. Botpress is a lean startup; the founder will be in your loop. They will scan every answer for which diagram you carry.

**Berkun's "no one cares":** *"As a leader I was up to my neck in problems. If someone showed up with a solution, they got support. If they asked for seats at tables, they were easier to ignore."* Translate every "design should…" instinct into "here's the business problem I'd solve."

### Before / after, using your actual projects

| Craft-language (caps you at senior) | Business-language (reads as Head-of-UX) |
|---|---|
| "At Acies.ai I designed the BI platform from scratch." | "At Acies.ai I was the founding designer on a BI platform for payments — the design *was* the product the company was selling; I was designing the thing customers paid for, in-room with the CEO." |
| "At Sprig I designed the AI survey-creation flow." | "At Sprig I designed AI survey creation so non-researchers could run real studies — the design problem and the company's expansion problem were the same problem." |
| "At Pendo I redesigned Data Explorer." | "At Pendo I redesigned Data Explorer — a surface where small design decisions get amplified across thousands of enterprise seats, so the craft *was* the retention lever." |
| "I'd improve Botpress onboarding." | "Botpress's growth bottleneck is non-developers reaching first-working-agent; that's a design problem that maps straight to activation and CAC payback." |

**The P&L move** (`business-impact.md`): learn to say which line your work moves. For Botpress, an agent platform: time-to-first-working-agent → activation → CAC payback; support-ticket UX failures → COGS; prototype-before-eng → R&D OpEx; designer retention → HR OpEx. You don't need to be a finance person — you need to name the line.

**The Irina-Stanescu lesson** (her first promo was rejected — *"I just took whatever tasks were given"*): your 30-60-90 should not be "listen, then ship a quick win." It should be **"find which line of Botpress's business is bleeding and route design there."** Reframe #97 accordingly (see §5).

## 4. The design exercise just got a rigorous upgrade

A hands-on "Head of UX" at an AI startup will very likely get a **whiteboard or take-home design exercise** — possibly an agent-UX prompt. The earlier prep was light here. Use the canonical **5W1H + 7-step framework** (book-05, the same book you had me read first):

**5W1H:** Why → Who → When/Where → What → How.
**7 steps, 1-hour budget:** Goal (3m) · Audience (5m) · Context+Needs (5m) · Ideas (5m) · Prioritize via impact/effort (3m) · **Solve (30m — 50%+)** · Measure/KPIs (3m) · Review (5m).

**Likely Botpress prompts** to rehearse with this framework:
- "Design the first-run experience for a non-developer building their first agent."
- "Redesign the agent-testing / evaluation experience."
- "Design how a builder debugs an agent that's failing in production."
- "Improve activation for non-technical users." (business-metric type)

**The 3 meta-tips you owe the interviewer:** clarify the task/deliverable; state assumptions out loud (Botpress's two audiences — builders vs. the agent's end-users — is the B2B2C trap from book-05; name it explicitly); present pros AND cons of your solution.

**The interviewer's 12-item evaluation checklist** (book-05 ch.4) — self-audit every answer against it: asks questions / takes feedback / communicates clearly / follows a process / valid assumptions / connects to company mission / target audience / context+needs / justifies idea selection / strong AND weak sides / explores beyond obvious / defines success metrics.

**The "one pushback" test:** interviewers deliberately push back once to see if you defend or incorporate. When they push, your move is *"good point — here's how that changes it,"* not re-arguing your first answer. Rehearse this reflex.

## 6. The fit verdict — sharper now

`career-stages.md` gives a data-backed decision input your earlier prep didn't have:

- **Management is ~75% meetings** (6–12/day at Meta); staff ICs average ~3/day. It takes **1–2 years to know if management fits**. Your bio explicitly wants hands-on work and a real team, not solo-contracting.
- **The honest read of Botpress "Head of UX":** best case it's *lead-as-IC + light mentoring at a design-led startup* (great fit — it's the founding-designer pattern you've thrived in at Acies.ai and MeetnGreetMe). Worst case it's *a solo designer with a grand title and no design org behind it* (this is the lone-designer trap your bio names as a deal-breaker, wearing a "Head" hat).
- **The deciding question isn't "can I do it" — it's "is the design org real."** You can do it. Whether you *want* it depends entirely on the answers to the §1 recruiter-screen questions.

**My recommendation:** treat the first conversation as *your* diligence, not just theirs. If there's a leveling doc, ≥2 other designers, and a design-background founder → strong yes, and your founding DNA makes you a top candidate. If it's "you'd be our first design hire as Head of UX" → that's a founding-designer role mislabeled, and you should price it (equity, title, autonomy) like one and decide against your bio's deal-breakers, not against the title's flattery.
