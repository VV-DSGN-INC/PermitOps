# Round 2 — Detailed Answers (Director of Product + PM)

First-person, in your voice (direct, concrete, modest-on-craft, confident-on-outcomes, no corporate-speak, no invented metrics). Audience is two non-designers from product. Each answer is ~60–120 seconds spoken. *Italics = what they're really asking + delivery note.*

---

### Q1. "Tell us about yourself / why this role?"
*Are you the self-starter who can own design with no playbook?*

> "Ten-plus years, mostly building B2B SaaS, analytics, and AI tools — and the pattern in my career is that I've been the first or founding designer three times. At Acies.ai I built a BI platform for the payments industry from zero, sitting in the same room as the CEO and the engineers. I co-founded a travel marketplace, MeetnGreetMe, that won a UN World Summit Award. And most recently at Sprig I led design for their AI research and reporting work and ended up owning the design system because it needed it.
>
> So 'first design leader, no playbook, has to ship as well as lead' isn't a stretch for me — it's the exact situation I keep choosing. That's why this role is interesting. It's not a bigger version of a job I have; it's the job I do best, at a company where the design problem actually matters."

*Delivery: 60 seconds, then stop. Don't list every job.*

---

### Q2. "Tell us about an ambiguous problem you identified before solving it."
*They asked a version of this in round 1. Tighter this time.*

> "At Sprig — they're a product-research platform moving into AI. I have a habit of clicking every button in a product before I design anything. Doing that, I found their feedback engine was broken in a way nobody had noticed: when a user gave feedback inside the AI chat, the system recorded the answer but not which message it was about. So every piece of feedback was context-free.
>
> The reason that mattered: we were building AI-assisted research on top of that exact feedback data. The foundation the new AI work depended on was quietly useless. Nobody flagged it because it didn't *look* broken — it only broke if you actually traced one piece of feedback end to end.
>
> I scoped the fix, we rebuilt the feedback link, and it unblocked the AI roadmap. The lesson I take from it: the highest-impact problems usually aren't on anyone's backlog, because nobody's looked closely enough to see them. That's the work I think a head of UX should be doing."

*Delivery: this is your strongest "I find problems others miss" story. 75 seconds. Land the last line.*

### Q3. "Here's the support product — how would you approach designing it?"
*Highest-probability exercise. Full script is in `support-product-exercise-script.md`.*

Open with two clarifying questions, name the B2B2C triangle, then run the **90-second spine**:

> "Botpress already wins on support, so the strategic job is making one human agent as effective as five — that's how you beat Zendesk and Intercom, because their AI is bolted onto ticketing and yours has no seam. Three audiences: the buyer is support leadership, the user is the agent, the end-customer is the B2C person — I design for the agent first because the other two follow. The real constraint isn't the AI, it's attention across eight to ten live threads. The hard problems: triage by risk not recency; an AI draft in the agent's voice that's one keystroke to accept and never auto-sends; trust through inline source not a confidence score; the AI-to-human handoff; and a monitoring layer that coaches instead of surveils. MVP is triage plus the draft loop — the rest is fast-follow. Success is handle-time down with CSAT flat or up, and I'd watch the draft-discard rate as the trust early-warning. Before building I'd prototype-test whether agents actually adopt drafts under pressure. Want me to go deeper anywhere?"

*Delivery: offer it before they fully ask — "want my quick take?" Then spine. Then stop.*

### Q4. "How would you work with me — I'm from product, not design, and I manage the designers today?"
*The handover. Will you partner or empire-build?*

> "Cleanly, and I'd want the first 30 days to be boring on purpose. I'm not coming in to relitigate decisions you and the team made — those were made with context I don't have yet. What changes is that design strategy and craft come off your plate and onto mine, and I feed it back to you in your language: tradeoffs, what we're shipping, what we're explicitly not doing and why.
>
> You stay focused on product strategy. I own design and make it faster and sharper. Amber and Jung-one keep continuity — same projects, no disruption — they just get someone whose full-time job is growing them and raising the bar. The way I think about it: trust starts around half and every interaction either charges or drains it. In month one I'd rather charge it by shipping something useful than by reorganizing anything."

*Delivery: this is the most important relationship answer in the loop. Warm, not territorial. ~75 sec.*

---

### Q5. "How would you grow Amber and Jung-one?"
*Is the team safe in your hands? Proof, not theory.*

> "First, I'd actually learn them before I change anything — deep one-on-ones, review their last few projects with them, find out what they each think they're stuck on. No reorg, no new process in week one.
>
> Then a few concrete things: weekly critique with a real format so feedback is structured, not vibes; one written growth goal per person, dated, that we revisit monthly; and protected focus time so mentoring doesn't fragment their maker hours. I learned that last one the hard way — I co-founded an outsourcing studio earlier in my career and managed four designers, mostly juniors. When juniors can interrupt you every ten minutes, nobody does deep work, including them. The fix is structure — office hours, a written 'default direction' doc — so questions get batched.
>
> What I'd point to as proof: the junior designers I mentored back then and at MeetnGreetMe — several are senior designers now, at places like PandaDoc and Booking. Growing people into the next level is the part of this job I find most rewarding, and I've actually done it."

*Delivery: the "they're seniors now at PandaDoc/Booking" line is your credibility. Don't skip it. ~90 sec.*

### Q6. "It's a player-coach role — how do you split hands-on vs. leading?"
*Will you disappear into management, or into craft, and neglect the other?*

> "Roughly 55% still doing the work, 25% coaching, 20% strategy and stakeholders. The hands-on stays high deliberately, for three reasons: three designers across 115 people means the leader has to ship real work, not just review it; juniors learn fastest by watching someone do the hard parts; and AI-agent UX is too new and too ambiguous to fully hand off yet — the patterns aren't set.
>
> That ratio should shift over time — more coaching as Amber and Jung-one level up, and especially once we add a mid-level designer, which I'd argue is the first hire we make, not the fifth, because that's the hire that buys back my time. The trap I watch for in myself is taking all the interesting ambiguous work and leaving the team the cleanup. So my rule is: if I'm about to pick up a piece of work, I ask whether someone could grow from it instead — and if yes, I pair with them rather than take it."

*Delivery: the numbers make it credible. ~75 sec.*

---

### Q7. "We move fast and you'll have 3 designers and a whole company asking. How do you prioritize?"
*Can you say no and route the team to what matters?*

> "By being ruthless about what actually moves the business, and saying no to the rest out loud. Right now that's the support product — that's where the company is betting, so that's where the design weight goes. Everything else gets triaged against it.
>
> The failure mode on a lean team is what I'd call snacking — low-effort work that feels productive and demos well but doesn't move anything. A leadership dashboard is the classic one: easy to build, easy to show, doesn't make a single support agent faster. I'd resist that even when it's the popular ask. Part of owning design is being the person who can explain, in product terms, why we're *not* doing the thing everyone's excited about this week — and being right often enough that people trust the call."

*Delivery: PMs respect "I say no well." ~60 sec.*

### Q8. "How do you do user research with no research team?"
*Will design be evidence-based or taste-based?*

> "Two layers. Continuous and lightweight, always on: I read support tickets, I'm in the community Discord, I watch session recordings, I read the YouTube and forum comments where people say what's not working. Yesterday, honestly, I was reading WordPress and Discord threads about a product I use, just tracing complaints. That's not a special project for me, it's a habit — it's the cheapest signal there is and almost nobody mines it.
>
> Then structured and deliberate before big bets: a focused study or prototype test on the riskiest assumption before engineering builds. For the support product that assumption is 'will agents actually trust an AI draft under time pressure' — I'd test that with real agents in a week, before we commit eng time. Research doesn't need a team; it needs the discipline to look before you build and the humility to be told you're wrong."

*Delivery: reframes your instinct as method. ~70 sec.*

### Q9. "Walk us through a project." (portfolio)
*Product thinking, or screen tour?*

Pick **InRule** for this audience — it's your closest parallel to Botpress.

> "InRule is enterprise decision automation — think rules and logic that run a business's regulated processes. The core problem: it was powerful but built for developers, and the company needed non-technical business people to be able to build with it too, without dumbing it down for the developers who relied on it. That's a hard dual-audience problem.
>
> What I owned: modernizing the builder interfaces, prototyping a next-gen unified platform experience, and contributing the design-system foundations. The decision I'm proudest of: instead of two modes — a 'simple' one and an 'advanced' one — we layered it, so a non-technical user and a developer worked in the same surface at different depths. The rejected alternative was the mode toggle, which is the obvious answer and the wrong one — people get stuck in the wrong mode and feel like switching means starting over. We also started using AI to generate the object schema from plain description, then let the user confirm or edit — early version of exactly the pattern that matters now.
>
> I'm walking you through InRule specifically because it's the closest thing I've done to your problem. An agent builder trending toward non-technical users *is* the InRule problem. I've shipped against it."

*Delivery: four beats — problem, your contribution, key decision + rejected alternative, why-this-project. Then the Botpress tie. ~95 sec. This is your strongest card; lead with it if you get to choose.*

### Q10. "Tell us about a disagreement with product or engineering."
*Do you fight well, or fold / dig in?*

> "At Acies.ai I worked literally in the same room as the CEO and the engineers — disagreement was constant and fast. One that stuck: I'd designed a cleaner, simpler view of the analytics, and the engineers and CEO wanted the dense version because that's what the payments customers actually asked for. My instinct was the clean one. I was wrong, and the way I found out was by asking them to walk me through how a real customer used it, instead of arguing my version.
>
> The thing I took from it: disagreement is usually missing information, not opposing taste. Now my default is — make the case once, hard, with evidence, in private; if I lose, commit fully and ship; if it turns out I was right, bring it back as a learning, not an 'I told you so.' On a small team where you're in the room with the founder, that discipline is the whole game. People stop trusting the designer who has to win every argument."

*Delivery: shows you can be wrong gracefully — high-scoring. ~80 sec.*

### Q11. "You've been consulting a while. Why a permanent role now?"
*Will you actually stay? Are you running from solo work or toward a team?*

> "Consulting taught me to walk into ambiguity and own design end-to-end, fast, with no scaffolding — that's most of why I'd be useful here from week one. What it can't give me is a team to grow over years or a product to compound on. You ship something, the engagement ends, you don't see the second-order effects or watch a junior you mentored become a senior.
>
> I want the part consulting structurally can't offer: continuity. The same team, the same product, long enough that the design system, the people, and the craft actually compound. And honestly — I'm in Montreal, I'm a bit tired of working alone from home, and I do better around designers and engineers in a room. This role is permanent, it's a real team, it's an AI product where design decides whether it works. That's the thing I'd stay for."

*Delivery: pull toward, not push from. The Montreal/in-office bit aligns you with their setup — say it. ~75 sec.*

### Q12. "It's a brand-new role with no playbook. How do you operate?"
*Self-starter, or needs structure handed to them?*

> "That's the part I want, not the part I'm worried about. Every founding-designer situation I've been in had no playbook — Acies.ai from zero, MeetnGreetMe from an idea. My first move is always the same and it's not 'design something.' It's: figure out which business problem design should own right now. Then I build the process around that problem, not before it.
>
> Concretely, first 30 days here: learn the two designers, learn the product by using it hard, talk to sales and support and engineering, and find which number the company most needs to move — probably activation for less-technical users, or the support product landing well. Then design gets pointed there, and the rituals — critique, research cadence, the design system — get built in service of that, not as a generic 'set up design ops' checklist. Process for its own sake is how you look busy without moving anything."

*Delivery: ~75 sec. The "process in service of the problem, not before it" line is the one.*

### Q13. "What worries you about our product or strategy?" (if asked)
*Can you think critically without being negative?*

> "Two things, and I mean them as questions more than criticisms. One: the dual audience. Serving developers and non-technical users in one product is the right ambition and the hardest design problem there is — it's exactly the InRule problem — and the natural drift is toward whichever audience shouts loudest. I'd want to understand how that tension is being held on the roadmap. Two: AI products drift. The model changes, the market moves, and the product you're building in May isn't quite the one you're running in eighteen months. That's not a flaw, it's the nature of it — but it means the design system and the patterns have to be built to absorb change, not freeze a moment. Those are the two things I'd be paying attention to early."

*Delivery: critical + constructive, ends forward-looking. Never just negative. ~60 sec.*

## Universal delivery rules for this round

- **60–120 sec, then stop.** End with "want me to go deeper?" Let them pull. (Fixes the round-1 ramble.)
- **Business/product words, not craft words.** "Activation," "the bet," "what we're not doing" — not "hierarchy," "delight."
- **Name real projects every time.** InRule, Sprig, Acies.ai, MeetnGreetMe. Specificity is your credibility; you have no need to invent a single metric.
- **When pushed, adapt — don't re-argue.** "Good point — here's how that changes it."
- **Lead with InRule when you get to choose the example.** It's your strongest card for this exact company.
