## 1. Honest diagnosis — what went not-so-well

Ranked by how much each cost you.

### A. Rambling, unstructured answers (the #1 problem, and it's now confirmed across two interviews)
Read the interviewer's side of the transcript: "Yep." "Mhmm." "Makes sense." "Got it." Those are *politeness tokens, not engagement*. She wasn't leaning in — she was waiting for you to land. Almost every answer was a run-on that started before you knew where it was going ("Yes. Well, it depends on our projects, right? And because it's one story when…"). The substance underneath was often strong. She mostly couldn't find it.

This is the same problem flagged in the Botpress round-1 analysis. **Two interviews, same failure. This is the thing to fix — everything else is secondary.**

### B. You volunteered weaknesses, unprompted, to an AI-first company evaluating you on AI
You said about Sprig: *"frankly was kinda behind in all this AI"* and *"there might be less research and validation that I wanted."* They explicitly told you the bar is "very AI-centric" and that they're *replacing a designer for not being AI-modern enough.* You handed them the exact disqualifier, unasked. This is the single most damaging unforced error in the transcript. (Same pattern as the Botpress comp answer — "ChatGPT/Gemini said 180." You keep narrating your own doubts out loud.)

### C. You missed the biggest opening in the interview
You asked "why are you replacing the old designer?" Good question. Her answer was a gift: *the current designer is strong on data-heavy design but their patterns are outdated, not AI-centric, don't match our vision.* That is the **exact bar you need to clear** — and you said "makes sense" and moved on. You had the ammunition to clear it (you run 16 agents on OpenClaw, build your own agent observability, use Claude+Figma daily for design systems, have Gen-AI certs). You fired none of it. You let the defining question of the interview pass.

### D. "Tell me your story + one AI product" became a chronological spray
She asked for a *story* and *one* data-heavy AI product. You gave: "15 years… 5 years full-time… 5 years co-founder… 5 years Toptal, 22 projects," then blurred Acies.ai → InRule → Sprig together without ever cleanly landing one. She had to do the work of finding the relevant project. The interviewer should never be the one assembling your narrative.

### E. Portfolio was a screen tour
"This is Sprig… this is InRule… this is the last one, brokerage automated with AI." She responded "these designs look really good" — surface-level, because you showed surfaces. You never made her *think* about a decision.

### F. "Where do you like to get involved?" → "it depends" with no spine
Two minutes of pods/CTOs/double-diamond/iterative before you reached an actual answer. "It depends" with no crisp default reads as *no strong point of view* — fatal for a role that wants someone to "own this strategically."

### What was genuinely good (keep and sharpen)
- **The trust/skepticism answer.** Reversibility, visibility, show sources, show step-by-step reasoning, surface underlying thinking without overwhelming. That is *exactly* Sleuth's core problem (skeptical expert analysts who must trust AI output). This was your best moment — it was just delivered messily.
- **The research depth.** Bought books on predictive marketing/retail, G2 + forums, competitor videos, RFM analysis, 20 features → narrowed to 3 with the CEO. Strong substance, weak delivery.
- **Founding-designer + PM-hybrid range.** Relevant to "own this role." Real.

## 2. The one structural fix (applies to every answer, every interview)

**Headline first. Then three beats. Then stop.**

Before you open your mouth, say the conclusion in one sentence. Then support it with at most three concrete beats. Then stop talking and let them pull.

> ❌ "Yes, well it depends on the project, right, because it's one story when you build from scratch and another when…"
> ✅ "My default is to start at the problem, not the screen. Three beats: I get the business and user picture clear, I pressure-test scope before pixels, then I prototype and iterate with engineers in the room. On a fast team I compress the front of that, not the iteration. Want the Acies.ai example?"

15 seconds vs. 2 minutes. Same content. One of them she can follow.

Drill: for every question in §4, the first sentence is the answer. Everything after is optional depth she has to ask for.

---

## 3. Three unforced errors to never repeat

1. **Never volunteer a weakness.** Not "I was behind on AI," not "less research than I wanted," not "ChatGPT told me 180." If asked about a gap, answer with what you did about it, not the raw doubt. You can be honest without narrating your insecurities.
2. **Never answer "it depends" without a default.** Always: "My default is X. It flexes to Y when Z." A POV with an exception reads senior; "it depends" reads junior.
3. **Never tour the portfolio.** Every project = one decision, the alternative you rejected, why. If they want the screen tour they'll ask.

## 4. Fixed answers for the CTO/CEO round (Sleuth-specific)

The CTO/CEO will care about: real AI fluency (not buzzword), data-heavy product judgment, the trust problem (their core UX risk), speed, and "can this person own it." Positioning: **"I'm the AI-native, data-heavy product designer who makes skeptical experts trust AI output — and I live in AI tooling, I don't just design it."**

### Q. "Tell us about yourself / your story."
> "Fourteen years, almost all of it early-stage startups, building data and AI products from zero or rebuilding them. The one most relevant to Sleuth: I was the founding designer at Acies.ai, a BI platform for the payments industry — huge datasets, predictive AI, and the core challenge was getting expert analysts to trust insights a model produced. That's your problem. More recently at Sprig I was Senior Product Designer for Platform & AI — set AI direction, rebuilt the design system. And outside client work I ship AI-native products weekly with Claude Code — a node-based visual flow-builder for AI pipelines, my own agent command-and-observability tooling, a universal design-token system. So AI-centric isn't a skill I'm adding; it's how I already work."
*Three sentences of arc, one project named as the bridge, one differentiator. ~45 sec. Stop.*

### Q. "Why you, versus the designer we have?" (or implied)
You must say this even if not asked directly — work it in.
> "You said the gap with the current designer is AI-modern patterns and matching where the product is going. That's exactly my edge. I don't design AI products from the outside — I live in them: sixteen agents on OpenClaw, my own reliability tooling, Claude wired into Figma daily for design-system work, Gen-AI design certifications. I bring the data-heavy depth you already have *plus* the modern AI-pattern fluency you said is missing. That combination is the whole reason this role exists."
*This is the most important answer in the round. It directly clears the stated bar. Rehearse it most.*

### Q. "Walk me through an AI / data-heavy product." (Acies.ai — your strongest mirror of Sleuth)
> "Acies.ai — founding designer, BI for payments. The problem: analysts drowning in transaction data, needing predictive insight they'd actually trust. Three beats. One: I did real research — read the domain, mined G2 and forums for competitor pain, ran RFM analysis on the data myself. Two: I came back with twenty possible features, and with the CEO we cut to three that mattered — that 20→3 cut is the work, not the twenty. Three: the first report builder was constrained by what engineering could ship; over a few iterations it became a flexible slice-and-dice tool. The thing I'd highlight for you: the hard part wasn't the dashboard, it was making a skeptical analyst trust a prediction. That's the same problem Sleuth has."
*~75 sec. The last line ties it to them. Then stop.*

### Q. "Our users are skeptical expert analysts who must trust the output. Have you designed for that?" (your best card — sharpen it)
> "Directly, twice — Acies.ai and Sprig. The pattern that works isn't 'make the AI look smarter,' it's making it *legible and reversible*. Four moves: every output traces to its source on click; the reasoning is shown step by step, not just the answer; the user can always revert to a previous state; and you surface the underlying thinking *on demand* without drowning them in it by default. Expert users don't want less AI — they want to audit it on their terms. For Sleuth specifically, the knowledge graph is an asset here: 'this insight came from these nodes' is a trust primitive most AI tools can't offer. I'd lean on that hard."
*This is the answer that wins the round. It's their exact problem and you've solved it. Tight, structured, ends on a Sleuth-specific insight.*

### Q. "How do you incorporate feedback / metrics and rethink something?"
> "Concrete example from Sprig: we shipped the AI survey-creation flow, watched usage, ran interviews, and found people didn't trust the AI-generated output enough to ship it as-is. So we changed the model from 'AI generates, you accept' to 'AI drafts, you see why, you edit, it learns.' Reframing the human's role from approver to editor was the fix. The principle I take everywhere: when adoption stalls on an AI feature, it's almost always a trust problem wearing a usability mask."
*Reframes the Sprig story as a strength — the opposite of round 1, where you said "I was behind on AI."*

### Q. "How do you work / where do you get involved?" (kill the "it depends")
> "Default: I get in at the problem, before requirements are frozen — that's where design changes the outcome instead of decorating it. Three beats: clarify the business and user picture, pressure-test scope, then prototype and iterate live with engineers. On a fast team like yours I compress the front and never the iteration. I've worked in six-person pods and solo-with-the-CTO; I adapt the ceremony, not the spine."
*~40 sec. A spine with an exception. Done.*

### Q. "Why this role / why Sleuth?"
> "Two reasons, specific. One: it's the exact intersection I've spent fourteen years in — data-heavy, AI-native, trust-critical, fast. Acies.ai and Sprig were rehearsals for this. Two: the knowledge-graph foundation means the trust problem is actually *solvable* here, not just patchable — most AI tools can't show their work; you can. That's a design problem I want."

## 5. Questions YOU ask the CTO/CEO (round 1 yours were thin — fix that)

- "Replacing a designer mid-stream is disruptive — what does 'clearly the right call' look like 90 days in?"
- "The knowledge graph is the trust differentiator. How exposed is it in the UI today, and how far do you *want* to expose it?"

- "What's the riskiest assumption in the product right now that design could de-risk?"
- "You ship fast — what's the most recent thing you shipped that you'd now redo, and why?"

Ask the same one of the CTO and the CEO. Mismatched answers tell you where the real risk is.

## 6. The cross-cutting point (this is why this analysis matters most)

The Botpress and Sleuth problems are the **same four habits**:
1. Starting answers before you know the landing → ramble
2. "It depends" with no default → reads junior
3. Portfolio as tour, not decision → no thinking shown
4. Volunteering doubt/weakness unprompted → hands them the rejection reason

Fixing these four fixes *every* interview you're in, both processes at once. The single drill that fixes all of them: **write the first sentence of each answer in §4, say it out loud timed, and physically stop talking at the end of beat three.** Record yourself once — you'll hear the rambling immediately, and that's the fastest correction there is.
